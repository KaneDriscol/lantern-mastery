This week we ran a Fight of the Braggart at practice. If you haven't seen one, the concept is simple enough on paper: one fencer stands in the middle, and the goal is for that person to fight and beat every other fencer at practice, one at a time. Once you've been beaten by the braggart in the middle, you step out. The scenario doesn't end until everyone's out or the braggart in the middle gives up.

Simple, on paper. In practice, this can become a little complicated.

## The Situation

I took over leading the scenario and to call out the next combatant to fight the braggart. This kind of scenarios usually wrap up in 3-6 rounds, in my experience. That way if you’re eliminated in the first round, you don’t usually have to wait that long before we move on and you get back into the action.

Unfortunately, this took much longer that 6 rounds. I honestly lost count because after about round 7-8 we were down to just three remaining fighters who just kept winning and everyone else was stuck watching.

Watching fencing isn't a bad way to spend an evening, but some of the people sitting on the sidelines had been waiting 30 minutes or more for the scenario to end. By the time those last three fencers had gone around 3 times, it was clear something had gone wrong with the shape of the thing. The fencer in the middle was tired, tuckered out, and inexperienced enough that they simply couldn't get through the three who were left. So it just kept going. And going.

On paper, the rule was clear: one-on-one, until the braggart wins or gives up. Followed to the letter, this could have gone on for another hour with no guarantee it would end.

So I changed the rules.

## Changing the Rules Mid-Scenario

We’re essentially trying to address two red flags that aren’t accounted for in the rules of the scenario:

1. The amount of time that someone may have to wait after being eliminated.
2. No built in way to control the end of the scenario in a satisfying way.

If you interrupt the scenario and call it, than that’s not really satisfying for the braggart who the scenario is set up to be celebrating. If you don’t find a way to end the scenario, those on the sidelines are stuck watching someone else have all the fun.

You might say that those eliminated could just leave and do something else, but this is the trap of the Fight of the Braggart. It’s a celebration of an individual in the group. If you leave, it’s kind of rude. So now you’re stuck.

So how to fix it?

Instead of holding to one-on-one until someone gave up, I started calling out additional fencers to face off against the three still on the outside — to tire them out and make it harder for *them* to stay in the fight, not just harder on the one in the middle. I escalated it. One-on-one became two-on-one, then three-on-one, then four-on-one, until finally the fencer in the middle broke through and beat all three.

I made it a little unfair for those on the outside to continue fighting, in hopes of making it easier for the braggart to defeat them. In the end it worked. 

{% principle "Rules Don’t Always Survive Contact with the Enemy" %}
The rules are written at the start, for the situation you expect. They don't always survive contact with the situation you actually get. You may need to adapt.
{% endprinciple %}

That idea isn't new to me. I've known it a long time, in the same way you learn that a tactic rarely survives its first real exchange with an opponent. A plan is a best guess made before you had all the information. Once the situation is in front of you and it doesn't match the guess, holding onto the plan for its own sake stops being discipline and starts being stubbornness.

## Why This Matters Beyond the Scenario

I know rules are supposed to provide order, and mostly, that's a good thing. But ask yourself this: if you'd known, at the moment you wrote the rule, exactly how the situation was going to unfold — would you have written it the same way? Usually not. The rule was right for the picture you had *then*. The picture in front of you *now* is a different one.

{% drawer "Isn't changing the rules mid-scenario unfair to someone?" %}
It can be, if you change them to benefit one side or to end things arbitrarily. That's not what this is. The goal here was to serve everyone at practice — the fencer stuck in the middle, and the people on the sidelines who came to participate, not just to watch one person grind through an unwinnable position for another half hour. Change the rules to fit the moment and the people in it, not to pick a winner.
{% enddrawer %}

{% principle "The rules serve the moment. Not the other way around.", true %}
A plan written before the fight is a best guess. When the situation stops matching the guess, being disciplined isn't holding the line — it's noticing the mismatch and adjusting.
{% endprinciple %}

So the next time a scenario, a lesson plan, or a set of rules you wrote isn't serving the people actually standing in front of you, ask yourself the same question I had to ask this week: would I have written it this way if I'd known then what I know now? If not, change it.
