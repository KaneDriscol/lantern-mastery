By my very nature, I like to collect and look out for frameworks. That's just how I work — give me a good pattern and I'll find a way to apply it to three other things I care about before the day is out. So it probably won't surprise you that a lesson with my apprentice Yip about a block/attack combo turned, in my head, into something bigger than the combo itself.

We were working on her response to a strike to the left side — nothing too high, nothing exotic. And somewhere in the middle of drilling it, I realized I was describing something I'd recognize instantly if you swapped the word "fencing" for "automation." Trigger. Response. Train. That's the whole pattern, and it's the same one whether you're building a block/attack combo or building the fourth step of a workflow that fires every time a form gets submitted.

## Find Your Trigger

The first step isn't the response — it's figuring out what you're responding *to*. A trigger is something that happens regularly enough in your dueling that you know it's coming back. Not a once-a-season fluke. Not the one weird angle your friend does that nobody else does. The thing that shows up often enough that it's worth building around.

{% keyidea %}
You don't train for edge cases. You train for what comes up most of the time.
{% endkeyidea %}

That's the part people skip. It's tempting to plan for the impressive edge case — the flashy parry against the trick shot — but that's not where mastery actually pays off.

With Yip, the trigger was plain and unglamorous: a strike to the left side, not too high. Nothing dramatic. But it's going to happen in nearly every bout she fences, which is exactly why it was worth building around.

## Design a Response That Feels Good

Once you've got the trigger, you start developing what you want to happen when it fires. And here's the part I think gets undersold: the key isn't just finding a response that *works* — it's finding one that *feels good*.

I don't mean flashy. I mean the response should make you a little excited to pull it off. If a combo is technically sound but feels like a chore, you won't reach for it under pressure — you'll hesitate, and hesitation is exactly what a trigger-response pattern is supposed to erase. So find the version that works *and* that you want to do. Those two things have to line up, or the whole thing falls apart the moment it matters.

Then comes the practical question: is one response enough, or do you need a second or third step chained onto it? You don't want something so minimal it's fragile — a single move that only works if everything else goes perfectly. But you also don't want to over-engineer it into a five-step sequence that falls apart if step three gets interrupted. The goal is something you can lean on *consistently*, not something that only shines in ideal conditions.

## Train It. A Lot.

Here's where the automation comparison holds up best. You can identify a perfect trigger and design a beautiful response, and none of it matters until you've trained it into your hands. This is where you develop **celerity** with the move — not just speed, but the kind of readiness that doesn't need to think first.

That means drilling the motion during the week. During practice. At home. In your sleep, if you can manage it. Repetition is the part with no shortcut. The trigger tells you *when*, the response tells you *what*, but training is what makes the whole thing show up automatically when the strike actually lands instead of a half-second too late.

{% drill "Trigger, Response, Train" %}
1. Pick one trigger — something that comes up in most of your bouts, not a rare edge case.
2. Design a response to it with a partner. Test a few versions and notice which one you actually want to reach for.
3. Decide if that single response is enough, or if it needs one more step chained on to make it reliable.
4. Drill just that trigger and response, on its own, for a full week before you add anything else.
{% enddrill %}

## You Don't Need Very Many of These

As you build toward mastery, it's tempting to think you need an enormous library of triggers and responses — one for every situation you can imagine. You don't. You only need a handful. But that handful has to be developed over a long time, with a lot of repetition behind each one, not thrown together the week before a tournament.

{% principle "Trigger. Response. Train.", true %}
Find what comes up most of the time. Build a response you actually want to use. Then drill it until it shows up on its own — during practice, at home, in your sleep. That's the whole path, one combo at a time.
{% endprinciple %}
