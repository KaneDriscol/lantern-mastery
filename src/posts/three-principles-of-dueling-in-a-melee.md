In preparation for Woodland War, I wanted to work with Yip on an aspect of fencing that I feel many overlook. _Dueling in a Melee_. Yes, you heard me.

## The Situation

It sounds like a contradiction, but this kind of thing happens all the time in a melee. You will end up facing off against individuals throughout the battle and it’s important to know what to do, so that you don’t end up squaring off and missing on the opportunities that will present themselves.

So as we dive into these principles, picture yourself alone in the woods or perhaps the field. It’s just you. No squad. No line. Just your sword. When suddenly another fencer comes around the bend. You both know you’re about the fight. They tense up, raise their sword, and approach with lethal intent (or at least apprehension). 

_What do you do?_

The wrong answer would be to square off and fight them in the same way you might fight in the pavilion or in a bear pit. To be clear, I would define squaring off as:

{% keyidea %}
Approaching your opponent, straight on, with your sword up. You move in until you’re in range and you engage until one of you walks away and the other is dead.
{% endkeyidea %}

Sound familiar? Yeah, that’s a duel. You might even salute on your way in (which isn’t really necessary during a melee, by the way). So rather than squaring off, keep the following three principles in mind.

## Find Your Angles

When you square off, you’re approaching head on with your opponent and we do this by default because it feels fair. But I’m not looking for a fair fight in a melee. I’m hunting for every advantage possible, and approaching at an angle makes a big difference.

You see, when you square off, there aren’t really any “ripples” that occur. A ripple are the various adjustments that happen because of your own actions. Approaching head on, your opponent settles in and you fight. When you approach at an angle, they adjust. They may rotate. They may step to the side or back off. They might adjust their guard, look a different way, think about what’s going on, or walk right into a bush. Those rippling changes are where you’re going to find your advantages and opportunities.

{% drill "Watch the Ripples" %}
The goal of this drill is to notice the “ripples” or adjustments that occur during a fight because of angles. You’ll need a partner.

1. Find a space and give yourself some distance between each other.
2. Approach, but square off, meaning approach head on. Watch what happens. 

	In most cases, someone will settle in and let the other approach. There will be minimal adjustments made as you both come into contact and engage.
3. Reset and approach again, but this time at angle. Move in to either their left or right side. Watch what happens.

	As soon as you take an angle, your partner will start making adjustments. They may rotate in place. They may back up. They’ll do something. Those are ripples.
4. Reset and repeat the drill, changing the angles and locations. Notice how different angles change the fight. Sometimes a little. Sometimes a lot.

{% enddrill %}

As you become familiar with angles and what ripples they can cause, you can start being strategic and identifying the best ones to take. Which angle is going to put you on their weak side? Which angle is going to protect you more? Which angle will cause your opponent to slow down, speed up, or be in a disadvantageous position?

If you just line up to duel, you’re going to miss all of this.

## Keep Moving

This _“Keep Moving”_ concept is pretty self-explanatory but it requires that you probably break a few habits.

First of all, you need to remember that **you are under no obligation to start or finish any fight during a melee.** You are perfectly allowed to raise your sword, block an incoming shot, and keep walking by.

If you don’t think that you’re going to get the kill on someone, don’t fight them. Remember, this is all about when you’re out in the melee field on your own. It’s just you. So pick the battles that you’re confident you can win. This may mean you have to run away. That’s okay.

Falling back to the first principle, *Finding Your Angles*, can help with this as well. You’re going to get passed fencers who want to stand and fight you. If you square off, you’ll have to go through them. If you approach at an angle, try an angle that will get them to move aside. As long as you keep moving, you may only need to block one shot and then you’ll get by.

On the flip side, if you keep moving, you’ll often find opportunities that don’t normally present themselves otherwise. One of my signature strikes during a melee is a “Passing Strike.” It’s essentially	a single block/attack combo as I move past someone at an angle.

{% drill "Passing Strike" %}

1. With a partner, standing out of range, begin your approach.
2. At first, approach head on long enough that your opponent settles in to a squared off position, but as you’re coming into range, veer off at an angle.
	I prefer veering to the left so my opponent ends up on my right side.
3. Without stopping or slowing down, you’re either going to block an incoming shot or beat their sword aside. Whatever happens, you just need to address their blade in some way.
4. By this point, you want to essentially be side by side with your opponent. The strike you’re looking for is coming at a 90&deg; angle from where you’re facing.
	This shot is only possible if you keep moving. Stopping along the way will allow your opponent enough space to maintain their block, but move far enough and you’ll pretty much walk around their defense.
5. Finally, no matter if the strike lands or not. You keep moving. If your opponent is still alive, you may have to rotate so they don’t strike back or you’ll have to move away fast enough that you leave the engagement.

{% enddrill %}

Keep Moving also doesn’t only apply to physically moving around the field. You also needs to break down how you think and make decisions in a melee.

{% keyidea %}
No matter what situation you’re in, you have to resist the urge to settle in. Both mentally and physically. There will be more opportunities that present themselves if you keep moving. If you miss one, there is going to be another.
{% endkeyidea %}

The trap is not only your feet stopping, it is your mind stopping too. Bogging yourself down wondering if you should go left or right, kill this one or slip past, what angle to take, etc… is just slowing you down. Make the call and go. Any call, and go. More chances are coming. They always come, but only if you're already moving through the field to meet them.

## Turn It Back into a Melee

The last one ties the other two together and reframes the whole thing: turn it back into a melee as fast as you can. A single fight inside a melee is not a place to settle into, it is a situation to resolve so you can get back to the real fight. And the fastest way to win a fight is for it to *not* be one-on-one. So look for an ally. Make it two-on-one, or better, pull the whole thing back to where your line has the numbers. Lead them toward your own side as it merges back up. Reposition so the flow of the melee closes around the two of you. All that angling and all that movement from the first two, this is partly what it is *for*. You're not only surviving the duel, you're steering it back toward the mass where the odds tilt your way.

So stay aware of the whole field even while I am locked in with one person. Watch where bodies are moving. Watch where our line is. And whatever it takes, move, rotate, break off, lead them, get back into the melee.

## Questions

{% drawer "How does this work if you’re in a line?" %}

If you’re in a line, you need to play by line fighting rules. These principles may be helpful, generally, but these three principles apply mostly when you’re out there fighting on your own. As soon as you’re back in a group, follow the best practices for the melee situation, which may be squad tactics, line tactics, etc...

{% enddrawer %}

{% drawer "Are there times that I should stand my ground as a single fighter?" %}
Yes. Absolutely. But only do so with a particular purpose in mind. 

Common reasons why you might stand your ground include:
- If you’re the only one defending a point.
- You’re attempting to delay the opposing team long enough for reinforcements to arrive.
- You’re trapped and have no where else to go
- Doing otherwise would break the “We’re Losing Light, Get Back in the Fight” rule. 

{% enddrawer %}

{% drawer "What if no one is around to turn the fight back into a melee?" %}
If there is literally no one around, and you absolutely have to fight someone one-on-one, you can still turn it back into a melee. Use the terrain to your advantage. Pull the fight back into a space where the currents of the melee might run into your duel. Just bide your time as long as you can. If a change of location or a ten extra seconds might drastically improve your odds, do it.

{% enddrawer %}

{% drawer "How risk averse should I be when on my own?" %}
This depends on the situation. If you’re in a melee with unlimited lives and the Rez point is near by, then go for it. Take a risk. If you only have three lives, are the MVP, the Rez point is super far away, or you’re charged with defending a key location, then be more cautious. Getting yourself killed trying to finish someone off may not be in your best interest.

{% enddrawer %}

{% drawer "Is it okay to avoid certain fighters when I’m running around solo?" %}

Yes! You don’t have to fight everyone you come across in a melee. Especially one-on-one. One of the first rules of melees is that numbers win. That’s how you take down bigger, scarier, more experienced fencers on the melee field. Throw numbers at them. But you don’t have numbers when you’re flying solo. So feel free to run away. Hunt down the straggles you feel you can take down. Attack the weak sides. If you’re following principle three, you should be able to get back into the melee and help take down bigger groups and more experienced fencers.

{% enddrawer %}
