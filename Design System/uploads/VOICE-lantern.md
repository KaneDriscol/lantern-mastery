# Lantern Voice Principles

## Hard rules

These are non-negotiable. Everything else in this document is guidance; this section is enforcement. Check copy against these before anything else.

**No em dashes.** Use commas, colons, periods, hyphens, or parentheses. A sentence that wants an em dash usually wants a colon or a period. If a pivot needs marking, a comma before "but" or "because" does the same work.

**No AI-giveaway phrases.** Cut "unleash," "game-changing," "dive into," "it's worth noting," "at the end of the day." Cut contrast crutches of the form "X doesn't just do Y, it does Z." Write the specific thing instead. If a technique is fast, say how fast and when that matters. Don't reach for a shape that sounds like emphasis and carries no content.

**No corporate jargon.** No "synergize," "leverage," "circle back," "touch base." This voice belongs to a fencing hall, not a conference room.

**Write for the ear.** If a line reads like documentation, rewrite it until it sounds natural said aloud. Read it out loud if you're unsure. A sentence you'd never say to a student at practice doesn't belong in a Lantern surface.

**No unearned superlatives.** Skip "best," "only," "first," "revolutionary" unless the claim is provable and you're prepared to prove it. Historical claims especially: "one of the earliest surviving" is honest, "the first" usually isn't.

## The core posture

Every piece of copy in a Lantern surface comes from the same person: a long-time practitioner and teacher writing for fellow fencers and students. Not a brand. Not a documentation team. A person with earned, humble authority who would rather be clear and warm than clever.

This posture holds whether the copy is a 2,000-word article on the cardinal virtues or the three words on a button. The register shifts; the person doesn't.

The single most important rule beyond the hard rules above: don't try to impress. The clearest tell of off-voice copy is prose reaching for effect, whether that's manufactured drama, a too-clever hook, or a "profound" one-liner that wasn't earned. Authority here is quiet and comes from experience and care. When a sentence feels like it's performing, cut it or flatten it.

## Two registers

Lantern copy operates in two registers. Know which one you're in.

### Long-form register
Articles, technique explanations, principle write-ups, historical context, reflection pieces. This is the fullest expression of the voice: first person, personal hooks, metaphor, room to think out loud.

### Interface register
Navigation, buttons, labels, form hints, empty states, error messages, card descriptions, tooltips. Shorter and more functional, but still recognizably the same person. Interface copy is the voice compressed, not the voice replaced.

The failure mode is treating interface copy as a neutral zone where generic product language creeps in. "Get started" and "Explore our resources" are not this voice. "Start with the fundamentals" and "Where will you begin?" are.

## Sentence rhythm

This is the part most likely to come out wrong.

The default sentence is **medium-to-long and builds a thought**. It carries the reader through an idea using a clause or two, pivoting on a single "but," "because," or "so." The key word is **variety**. Building sentences mix with medium ones and the occasional short one.

The short punchy sentence is rare and earns its impact precisely because it follows longer building sentences. It snaps the built-up thought shut. It is punctuation, not the melody.

**Target ratio:** for every short punchy sentence, two to four longer building sentences leading into it. "Longer" means varied and well-constructed, not run-on. A good building sentence has **one main pivot, not three**.

### Two failure modes, equally bad

1. **Stacked short sentences.** ("And that got us talking. Because here's the thing. Waiting isn't passive.") Reads as an AI performing drama.
2. **Run-ons chained with "and... and... and."** Or three-plus clauses crammed together. If a sentence has more than one "and" doing structural work, or runs past roughly 30 to 35 words without a clear pivot, break it in two.

Long sentences are allowed to ramble a little and self-correct mid-stream, the way someone thinking out loud does. Don't over-tighten these into clean, balanced clauses. A bit of looseness is human.

**Rhythm check before shipping any long-form copy:** reread once just for rhythm. Count short sentences in a row, since a run of them needs longer lead-ins. Scan for run-ons and break any sentence with more than one structural "and."

In interface register, rhythm compresses but doesn't disappear. A card description of two sentences should still have shape rather than reading as two clipped fragments.

## Distinctive moves

These are habits, not rules. Good Lantern copy contains most of them naturally.

**The immediate softener.** When the copy says something blunt, walk it back one step. Not to retract it, but to stay relational. This is copy written for people you'll see in person.
> "That's the wrong question, though. And I don't mean that harshly, it's the obvious question."

Never let a correction land without this beat. It isn't hedging; it's community awareness.

**Telegraphing the move.** Name what's about to happen before it happens. A teacher habit: keep the reader oriented so they're following the idea rather than catching up to it.

**Italics as precision, not decoration.** Italicize the exact word carrying the conceptual weight, the word the sentence is pointing at. Not for drama; for accuracy.
> "Those are descriptions. They're what you're *observing* when you watch mastery in action."
> "not just be fast but be fast *at the right moment*"

If removing the italicized word wouldn't make the sentence miss its point, don't italicize it.

**The section-closing theorem.** After building a point across a paragraph or two, land it with a short plain declarative, almost like writing "QED."
> "The Eye is what it looks like from the outside."
> "That's the pattern."

These aren't punchy for effect. They're the point, stated simply after everything else was the proof.

**Including yourself in the mistake.** When correcting a misunderstanding, fold yourself into it. Never lecture down.
> "here's where we started getting a little turned around, and honestly, I include myself in that"

If a piece is correcting something, it should almost always contain a version of this move.

**The live teaching example.** Ground abstract ideas in a real moment: present-tense feel, specific detail, the actual conversation. Name the person, name the technique, name the moment. This is different from a generic "in practice, students often..."

**Ladders.** Chained progressions that land a point ("Doing leads to learning. Learning leads to knowing. Knowing leads to confidence.") Distinctive and effective, but use at most once per piece, as a closing flourish rather than a default move.

**Question clusters.** Building a concept through stacked questions ("Can you fight without flinching? Can you stay composed when what you need to do is wait?") A specific tool for defining something. Don't reach for it in every section.

## Imagery and motifs

**Woodland, growth, seeds, tending.** The core metaphor family: confidence as something you nurture and tend, ideas as seeds of study that resonate and inspire years of work. Reach for this when a piece needs a unifying image.

**The itch, the pull of the season.** Fencing as something that calls to you: the return to the woods, the clang of metal, the laughter of friends.

**Compass, paths, journeys.** "My fencing journey," "a path to pursue," "where will you start?"

Use these as seasoning, not the meal. Overused, they become cliché and the prose starts performing again.

## Structural patterns

Long-form pieces almost always take one of three shapes. Match the shape to the content rather than forcing a framework onto a reflection.

**The Framework piece.** Personal hook establishing why the idea grabbed you, then what sparked it (usually crediting a source), then the framework in named sections with each defined in one or two tight paragraphs, closing with a turn outward toward how you're pursuing it.

**The Study piece.** A question or hypothesis to test, then the method in plain terms, then findings section by section with genuine surprise where earned, closing by tying the data back to a lesson or a person's wisdom.

**The Reflection piece.** A seasonal or personal hook (often a quote that stuck), then a reframe offering a few approaches, then each approach as its own section written as an invitation, closing with a question that hands it back to the reader.

## Openings and closings

**Openings never start cold on the topic.** Open with a personal trait or habit, the season or a mood, a quote that stuck, or a small study you've been itching to do. The hook establishes why this matters to you before getting to what it is.

**Closings turn outward and hand it to the reader.** A direct question ("Where will you start?"). An invitation or gentle charge ("So go ahead. Try it!"). A return to the opening image to close the loop.

In interface register, this translates to CTAs that invite rather than command. "Where will you begin?" over "Get Started." "Try this drill" over "Launch."

## Attribution and community

Credit sources and people generously, with titles. Titles matter: Captain, Captain Emeritus, apprentice. Use "we" when talking about the group and how we fence.

**Never fabricate.** Fencing history, treatise details, what a cited author said, dates, attributions: if a detail isn't verified, flag it as a placeholder rather than inventing it. This applies with particular force to historical sources, where a plausible-sounding invention does real damage to a resource meant to be trusted. It also connects to the hard rule on superlatives. Most "first surviving treatise" claims are contested, so write what's actually known.

## Do

- Let genuine surprise and enthusiasm show.
- Define terms plainly, then deepen them.
- Keep paragraphs readable. A few sentences each, one idea per paragraph.
- Admit the limits of an idea honestly ("This won't fix a bad measure problem").
- Write for fellow fencers and students, not a generic internet audience.
- Use **bold** for key terms the first time they land, and for numbers that are the punchline of a study.
- Use block quotes for an attributed quote that anchors a piece, attribution on its own line.
- Write section headers that are evocative or a turn of phrase, not just labels: "A Path to Pursue," "Heed the Captain," not "Section Three: Techniques."

## Don't

- Don't be clinical or corporate. No "In this article, we will explore..."
- Don't try to impress. Worth repeating, since it's the most common failure.
- Don't over-hedge or pile on disclaimers.
- Don't strip out personality to make copy "cleaner." The rambling-turned-clear quality is the point.
- Don't stack short sentences. Also worth repeating, since it's the most common mechanical mistake.
- Don't overuse the woodland metaphor.
- Don't let interface copy drift into generic product language.
- Don't blend in a procedural how-to register. Instructional content here stays reflective and community-rooted rather than becoming numbered-step documentation.

## Interface copy specifics

**Buttons.** Verb-first and specific. "Start the drill," "Read the source," "Log this session." Avoid "Learn More" and "Get Started," which belong to a different brand.

**Empty states.** An opportunity for the voice, not a dead end. An empty practice log isn't "No entries found." It's closer to "Nothing logged yet. The first entry is the hardest one."

**Error messages.** Apply the immediate softener. Name what went wrong plainly, then stay relational rather than clinical.

**Card descriptions.** One or two sentences with actual shape. Enough to tell the reader why this matters, not just what it is.

**Navigation and labels.** Plain and honest. The voice shows up in tone, not in cleverness. A nav item is not the place for a turn of phrase.

**Form hints.** Explain the reasoning, briefly. The teacher habit of anticipating the reader's question applies here as much as anywhere.

## Relationship to the design system

The Lantern visual system runs on cream canvas, serif display type at weight 400, and scarce bronze accent. The voice is the same restraint expressed in words: nothing bolded that doesn't need bolding, nothing dramatized that wasn't earned, warmth carried by care rather than by exclamation.

When visual and verbal decisions conflict, the shared principle resolves it. **Bigger and quieter before louder.** In type, that's a larger serif before a heavier weight. In copy, that's a clearer sentence before a more emphatic one.
