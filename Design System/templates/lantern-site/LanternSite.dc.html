I have a habit of showing up to practice early, before anyone else, just to stand in the empty hall for a minute. It is a small thing, and I don't make much of it, but that quiet is where most of what I understand about fencing has actually settled.

Last week an apprentice asked me why we drill the same cut for months before we ever spar with it. It is the obvious question, and I don't mean that as a dismissal, it is the question everyone arrives with. The honest answer is that speed without control is just haste, and haste loses to anyone patient enough to wait for it.

What the source actually says

Meyer is careful here, and I think we lose something when we skim past it. He does not tell you to cut fast. He tells you to cut so that you are already prepared for the answer, which is a different instruction entirely, and one that takes years to feel rather than understand.

So we drill. Not because repetition is virtuous on its own, but because it is the only way I know to move a technique from the front of your mind to the back of your hand. Doing leads to learning. Learning leads to knowing. Knowing is what lets you stop thinking about the cut and start reading your opponent.

That is the pattern.
